Internet, Society and Design Justice

Internet, Society and Design Justice

The research that I have engaged with for this writing all relate to the broad topic of Digital Equality and discuss the different factors that affect society’s current standing in relation to Digital Equality.

What is Digital Equality?

Digital Equality is the equal access and use of digital technologies[1][2]. This topic seems to be a branch off of the issue of the Digital Divide – the differences in access and use of digital technologies and the internet between different parts of the world.

What does Digital Equality Aim to Address?

Digital Equality aims to address the inequalities that have emerged from the Digital Divide. The world is becoming increasingly reliant on the internet to facilitate things such education, research, business, commerce, and communication. Lack of access to digital devices and internet results in lack of access to all of the above.

People around the world already face a lack of access to these things in the physical world and by not having access to them they face an increased disadvantage in the world compared to those who have access to both physical and digital resources[1][2][3].

The Covid-19 Pandemic has also demonstrated society’s increasing reliance on technology to be able to function[3][4][5]. Having access to digital devices such as laptops, tablets and smartphones, as well as access to internet were(and still are) vital to keep the world and the economy functioning in the Pandemic, since most forms of business and education were forced to switch to online alternatives to comply with social-distancing measures – meaning the ability to work and access to education became reliant on having a device and internet connection.

In instances like these, the most vulnerable, such as victims of poverty and unstable political situations, are the ones who suffer the most, since they cannot easily access the same resources as those who have financial privilege.

How is this addressed and implemented?

The ultimate goal is for issues, like the ones raised above, to be addressed through governmental policy and legislature, as well as funding and aid from private organisations and institutions[1][4[5].

In the case of the Covid-19 pandemic, governments and institutions around the world provided aid and resources in the form of devices and internet services to those in need so that they could access schooling and work resources.

Some examples include[3]:

  • The UK government providing loan devices for students in need
  • Students in Egypt being provided free SIM cards and subsidised internet costs
  • Mobile users in Thailand receiving free monthly data

Achieving Digital Equality?

So the issues raised above have been addressed through specific methods of increasing digital equality, such as education on how to use the internet, providing resources such as devices and internet broadband[3][4][5], but does this actually bridge the gap and create digital equality?

Providing programmes and resources to ensure people have access to digital devices and an internet connection is great, and this can help provide disadvantaged groups with greater opportunities, but this does not address the root issue of why Digital Equality does not exist in the first place, nor does it address how the level access to technologies affects people’s capacity to benefit from digital technologies.

I believe that the level of access that a person has to the internet and digital technologies directly increases the benefits that they can gain from using those technologies. A noted statistic is that “about 59% of the global population having access to the internet by the spring of 2020”[5], which clearly implies that a significant majority of the world has internet access, however this does not address the differences in level of access. I doubt that the majority of this portion of the population has unlimited/uncapped internet access, and the likely scenario is that most have limited budget of data that they can use every month. This context immediately changes how Digital Equality should be viewed.

For example, consider 2 students who both have to use online schooling as a result of the Covid-19 Pandemic, where one student only has a small tablet and receives 1GB of data a month, and the other student has a smartphone, laptop, and tablet and has uncapped Wi-Fi at home. Both these students have access to a device and internet and learning, so therefore are they not Digitally Equal? I disagree. The second student has more devices and greater access to the internet, and this gives them an advantage over the first student in being able to fully utilise online learning, since they do not have to worry about their device usage or their internet usage.

Even though this is a small scale scenario, it is useful in demonstrating that Digital Equality cannot simply undo the imbalances that already exist in society, since the underlying root cause has not been address.

I believe that Digital Equality is a result of economic inequality and the capitalistic system that world has adopted. Digital Equality cannot be truly achieved until the underlying economic inequalities that caused it are addressed.

Some examples of economic inequalities that need to be addressed before Digital Equality can be achieved are:

  • Equal access to healthcare
  • Equal access to electricity
  • Equal access to food and water
  • Equal access to housing

These are all issues that need to be addressed by governments to ensure that those who live in suffering and poverty can live better lives. These issues also directly affect how people can make use of digital technologies to their advantage, assuming they already have access to technologies.

For example, consider a specific South African context: What happens during load shedding and people do not have access to electricity for school and work? The privileged and rich can afford to use generators or alternative power solutions during these outages, and so they can continue to work and access learning material, whereas the poor do not have these same luxuries, and are forced to wait for the situation to be fixed. This difference in economic equality directly affects people’s ability to take advantage of the benefits that digital technologies can offer, and therefore hinders Digital Equality from being achieved.

Conclusion

Achieving Digital Equality is an important goal, especially in the digital age we live in where many of our services, resources, businesses and education are moving to digital platforms. Even though working towards solving digital inequality is important, and some of the programmes and aids given by governments are a great starting point to alleviate the issue, the only way to truly achieve Digital Equality is to ensure that governments also address economic inequalities and create policies and measures that address and fix these inequalities. Only then will Digital Equality become an achievable goal.
