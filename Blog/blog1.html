A reflection of Simon Harper’s reflection of “As We May Think”

Harper begins his reflection by recounting the importance that “As We May Think” had in creating new areas of study and research. He notes that some people consider the concepts from the text to be outdated, however he disagrees and thinks the concept of thought and association within human thinking is still relevant today.

Harper proceeds to discuss a machine that Bush created, called the Differential Analyser. It was a mechanical device that could solve mathematical differential equations. He discusses how the machine represented Bush’s way of thinking, taking abstract mathematical concepts and using physical means to demonstrate their operations and functions.

Harper goes on to discuss Bush’s proposal of the MEMEX, a mechanised system where information could be sorted and retrieved for an individual. He discusses Bush’s ideas on human minds linking thoughts through association as opposed to an arbitrarily sorted index.

He ends his reflection by praising Bush for providing his concept on how large scale information could be stored and managed for an individual to help them better understand and investigate new concepts in the world.

I believe that this text highlights two important details. The first being that people don’t think in a static and index-based thought process, but rather that our thoughts are interconnected and jump from one to the other based on some form of association from the previous thought. Secondly, using this association of thought can help us better interpret pieces of information and learn concepts more easily.

I think these two concepts are important when working in any sort of software-based framework, whether it be video games, apps, websites or simple programmes.

In terms of designing any of the above, I think it is useful to take the two points mentioned above and include them in the design process. When we use any form of software, we prefer when things feel intuitive and feel like they “make sense”. I think that one could take the concepts of association of thought to create a well designed user-experience.

We can see how these concepts are already applied to software that has a good user-experience. Websites that are easy to use will often have the navigation based in categories that are broken down into sub-categories that were related to the main category. For instance, a main category of Gaming could be broken into sub-categories such as Video Games, Accessories, Consoles, Chairs ect. This shows how you could navigate through the website through association of information.

In terms of how using this sort of association to learn concepts more easily can be shown in how many well-designed apps are designed with an intuitive interface. For example, if you use an app like Twitter, it probably won’t be too difficult for you to figure out how Reddit works and vice versa. Or if you use a messaging app like Whatsapp as well as posting-based app like Instagram, it wouldn’t be too difficult to figure out how SnapChat works. If you use multiple social media platforms, then you increase your “vocabulary” on how different apps and websites work, and in turn you can probably figure out how to use most other apps and websites, provided they aren’t designed horribly for the user.

I think we can also see that information on apps and websites are being grouped by attempted association more often now in an attempt to make the user-experience more fluid. For example, how Instagram uses an algorithm to bring up content you might like based on previous content that the algorithm has seen that you enjoyed.

I think its intriguing to see how Bush’s idea of grouping information through association is being used so many decades later, even if its not in the exact way he intended(such as memes and similar content on social media), and it is also clear that Harper was correct in stating that Bush’s ideas weren’t outdated since we clearly still do use them today.

References:
