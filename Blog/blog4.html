Close Reading of Graham et al’s “Toward A Study of Information Geographies”

A Close Reading of Graham et al’s “Toward A Study of Information Geographies”

1. Description

This is a summary and analysis of the paper “Toward A Study of Information Geographies”.

2.Summary and Breakdown of the Paper

The paper/Graham et al aims to collect to data about different information geographies and broadly analyse it to understand how information access and contribution on the web is distributed across the world.

2.1 Information Geographies, Immutable Mobiles and (Im)mutable Augmentations

The Paper refers to the concepts of Information Geographies, Mutability, Mutable Mobiles and (Im)mutable Augmentations.

Information Geography refers to the context, meaning and origin of a piece of information. Graham et al states that “it is from somewhere, about somewhere”. In other words, information has meaning, and its origin and form of representation also has meaning.

Mutability refers to the tendency of something to change. The paper assumes Immutability to be something’s inability to change.

Immutable Mobiles refers to information sets that can be physically moved across locations without “significant” change to its meaning. Their medium of representation is bound to a physical object and is also generally immutable. An example of this a book or newspaper, where the information is conveyed through print and paper.

Im(mutable) Augmentations refers to dynamic information sets with specific geographies but are not specifically mutable in their form or meaning. It can be both mutable and immutable. The information can be accessed across different locations without specifically needing to move the object. An example of this would be accessing an online article from a laptop.

The paper outlines the transition from the “era of print” to the digital age, where (Im)mutable augmentations resulted from an evolution from Mutable Mobiles.

It is explained that Im(mutable) Augmentations play an important role in the exposure and understanding of different places within the world. Representation of places and groups of people is also dependent on this.

2.2 Pre-Digital Geographies of Information

The ease of access to information in the Pre-Digital Era was limited, both in content and in location. The technological means of the time were not sufficient in allowing global mass contribution to information, nor could information be easily accessed across the world.

Information sources such as books and texts were limited to institutions such as Universities and Associations, which were not widely accessible to the general public – if you could not travel to access where the information was located, then you could not access it. This meant that “knowledge and codified information” were “produced under conditions of power…exerting economic social, and political power”.

The paper also points out that this meant that information sources such as books and patents were generally produced and also mainly about the Global North, whereas the Global South was the minority in this this production of information. This showed that the Global South had an unequal access to information and the socio-economic benefits that came with it.

2.3 Changing Geographies After The Information Revolution

The current period is considered an “Information Revolution”. The formation of digital technologies such as computers, which are more readily accessible now around the world, has led to an increase in the production, contribution and access to information.

The paper notes positive opinions about this era, such as Tapscott and Williams(2006) and Shirky(2011), who believe that this greater access and contribution to information across the web can allow for citizens to “play a more significant role in shaping content and augmentations that play key roles in their lives”. It also notes the opinion that “For the first time in a millennium, we have a technology to equalize the opportunity that people have to access and participate in the construction of knowledge and culture, regardless of their geographic placing”(Lessig 2003).

Graham et al seek to analyse global data to see if opinions like the ones above hold any truth. It is noted that 3 billion people have access to the global network, with 25 billion devices accessing that network. Graham et al also wish to use the data to see how this increased access to information affects political and economic geographies of information, as well at the participation and representation of information contribution.

To gain insight into this matter, Graham et all have conducted a research survey of “contemporary geographies key digital and internet-mediated processes”, which is split into the categories of:

  • Geographies of Access and Enablement
  • Geographies of Participation
  • Geographies of Representation

2.4 Geographies of Access and Enablement

This section of the paper details “who has access to the technologies and services essential for digital communication, participation and representation”. Access to the internet is required in order to participate in most forms of digital information contribution, therefore global scale data is required in order to investigate this section.

The paper makes use of population data sets collected by the World Bank to track internet users and connection per country. It also makes use of data sets from the International Telecommunication Union to track the cost of broadband as a percentage of average income in a country.

The main findings from the data were that:

  • Asia accounts for the largest section in the world for internet users, at 42%
  • Many of the largest internet nations have low a low percentage of internet users relative to their population
  • As a whole, Africa has low internet penetration and growth
  • The cost of broadband subscriptions is generally the highest in Africa.

It can be seen that generally Africa(as well as some other global south countries) has less access to internet than the rest of the world, and generally also has the highest broadband fees – making it further difficult to access the internet.

2.5 Geographies of Participation

This is investigated through three variables, namely, Domain Names, Distributions of Collaborative Coding, and Geographies of Wikipedia Contributions.

Domain Names are used to show the distribution of digital content around the world. The data set showed that 78% of domains are registered in North America and Europe, which indicates the “dominance…in internet content production”, that those regions hold.

GitHub is the chosen metric to measure distributions of collaborative coding due the fact that it is the largest “code hosting service in the world”. The data set showed over North America and Europe each make up over 33%(1/3) of GitHub users across the world. Asia makes up 17% of users, and Africa and the Middle East account for less than 1% of users. Graham et al also expressed that they expected the Africa and Middle East to have more GitHub users based on their level of internet access.

Wikipedia is “the world’s largest and most used encyclopaedia”, and allows for all internet users to contribute to it. The data sets showed that for a 2-year period, every 3 months, the United States, Germany, the United Kingdom and France each make over 1 million edits on Wikipedia. Comparatively, Africa and the Middle East only made a few thousand edits in the same time frame. Another data set was also used to track the percentages of origins of the Wikipedia edits for countries. The United States had 85% of edits made within its country, and similarly The United Kingdom had 78%. However, Africa showed low percentages, such as Nigeria with 16% and Kenya with 9%.

These numbers indicate that the Global North creates the majority of digital content.

2.5 Geographies of Representation

This investigates representation of the world on the internet as a digital space. Data from Google Search, OpenStreetMap and the Semantic Web is used to analyse this digital representation.

Google Search data was used to analyse digital representation per country, based on the number of mentions/webpages that appear for a country in Google’s Index. The data showed the United States had the most mentions. Graham et al state that this is indicative of the information inequality across the world.

“OpenStreetMap(OSM) is the world’s largest roadmap”. The data collected showed that the Global North accounted for the majority of all the content on the platform. The Global South is the minority of content, for example, Africa and Latin America each only make up 5% of the content on the platform.

Semantic Web refers to common frameworks that are used to improve data efficiency on the web. Google acts as the primary web interface for the majority of Americans and Europeans. Back-end databases are used to “ feed’facts’ ” into engines like Google. The most important databases for this is Freebase and Geonames. The data set analysed showed that the majority of content hosted on these databases is related to the United States more than any other country.

2.6 Graham et al Conclusions

The paper reviews the geographies related to internet access and contributions. The paper indicates that there is unequal distribution of access and contribution of information geographies. It also demonstrates that there is little evidence to support the notion that increased access to the internet and technologies creates an increase in participation, contribution and representation of information geographies. It is generally seen that the Global North(often specifically the United States) has greater access, representation and contribution to the internet than countries in the Global South.

The research conducted is broad starting point to further analyse the data collected. Further research and work must be conducted to analyse the context of the “uneven geographies”.

Aims

The aim of the paper was to analyse collected data(both existing data and data that had not been previously collected or analysed) to understand information geographies and how they are distributed, accessed, participated in and represented in a global context.

The aim is not to specifically gain any specific answer from the analysis of the data, but rather to see what questions are formed from the analysis, in order to create further work and study in the matter of information geographies.

Methodology

To investigate the distribution, access, participation and representation of information geographies in a global context, Graham et al:

  • Conducted a “broad-scale” global survey in three specific categories
    • Geographies of Access and Enablement
    • Geographies of Participation
    • Geographies of Representation
  • Collected data from established institutions and informants, such as Google, the International Telecommunications Union, and the World Bank
  • Made use of bespoke data tools to generate their own data
  • Analysed and provided statistical breakdowns of the data in both numerical and graphical forms
  • Referenced other bodies of work for information, data collection and analysis
  • Limited the analysis of the collected data to the scale of a country
  • Provided general analysis of the data between the Global North and South

This methodology approach was effective in collecting reliable and (mostly)accurate data, and it was also effective in displaying this data to the reader in way that could be effectively visualised and understood.

The use of world maps, colours and sizing was effective in displaying the different distributions of data between the countries.

Although this methodology is effective in obtaining global scale data, it is not effective in providing data that can be analysed to understand the specific reasons for the different distributions. This is noted by Graham et al in the paper, and they state that the specific analysis of this is reserved for future research.

Results

The results obtained by Graham et al show that in general there is uneven distribution of information geographies in the world, with the Global North tending to have greater access, distribution and representation compared to the Global South.

The results show that 3 billion people have access to the internet, but the majority of digital content contribution and access to it lies with the Global North.

The data obtained also shows that countries from the Global South(primarily African countries) have a high bandwidth cost.

Reflection on the Conclusions of Graham et al

Based on the results mentioned above, it is clear that the Global South is at a disadvantage in terms of benefitting from the “information revolution” compared to the Global North. The paper does a thorough job in collecting reliable global data to come to this conclusion, and I agree with Graham et al’s position that this general global data needed to be collected and studied. However, some of these results seem predictable when reading the paper from a South African perspective. Graham et al acknowledge that the paper was written and published in Western Europe and so it is written from that specific view point, however, it would seem obvious to me as a South African(without needing to read this paper) that the Global North has greater access to the internet as well as its benefits than those in the Global South. This observation also helps to highlight the differences in the information geographies, as it aligns with the data collected in the paper – such as Google’s mentions of the North and US being greater than that of the South and regions like Africa.

The conclusions drawn in this paper are important in highlighting the issue of uneven information geographies, and as Graham et al point out, opening this field of study can prompt further study and analysis to take place. Further research can allow for greater insight to be gained into the issues and allow for solutions to be created, as well as create greater awareness of the topic.

Additional Research and Related Topics

The Digital Divide[1][2]:

The research in the paper can be related to the research on the Digital Divide(the topic is even mentioned in the paper when discussing broadband costs).

The Digital Divide refers to the unequal access of technologies, such as computers and the internet, due to social and economic factors such as income, education, gender, and race. Briefly, individuals with more education, greater income and who don’t face discrimination due to race and gender generally have greater access to these technologies, and the benefits that come with it.

Research has been conducted which shows that the above mentioned factors affect individuals access to technology. The research being conducted by Graham et al could also make use of the research conducted for the Digital Divide to help give context and understanding to the overall national data sets that they have collected.

Web Crawlers[3][4]:

The paper mentions domain names are used to make content findable. The paper also mentions that Search Engines such as Google make use of back-end databases to provide results for search queries. This relates to the topic of Web Crawlers. Web Crawlers search through webpages to scan for metadata and information related to the search query. The use of Web Crawlers is what allows Google to bring up the search results for the mentions of the United States of America on webpages(as mentioned in the paper for collection of data).

It can be seen that Web Crawlers is an example of internet technologies that is used to help facilitate (Im)mutable augmentations on the internet, as it aids in the search, distribution and access of information for Search Engines on the internet.

Suggested Avenues for further research

Graham et al also state that this research was meant to open more topics of research and study, and that’s why they chose to specifically analyse the data at a country level. However, I believe that when studying the access and distributions of technologies such as the internet, it is also important to analyse economic factors of the countries involved. Factors such as the wealth of a country and their access to infrastructure also have an impact on the level access to the internet.

Further research should also be conducted into the different ways countries “contribute” to the information on the internet. Other metrics besides GitHub and Wikipedia contributions could provide further insight in how different regions contribute and participate in the internet. Potential avenues for this could be the amount of internet users in a country that use specific social media platforms such as Twitter and Instagram, as these are global platforms where users can contribute in expressing their opinions and identities.

Final Conclusions

Graham et al draw an important conclusion(and maybe even an obvious one) that there is an uneven distribution of information geographies, where the Global North tends to have dominance over the South. Further research as to why this occurs should have been conducted to understand the context behind the data
